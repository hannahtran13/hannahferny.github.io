Motivation

High accuracy doesn't mean structural understanding.

Modern transformer models achieve strong performance across sequence modeling tasks, but performance alone does not tell us whether a model has learned the rules governing a system, or merely approximated its surface patterns.

This research isolates that distinction by studying formal languages generated by deterministic finite automata (DFAs), where the true generative rules are fully known and unambiguous. By controlling the environment completely, we can ask precisely: what has the model actually learned?

Methodology

A controlled experimental environment
where the truth is always known.

All sequences were generated from deterministic finite automata, meaning the correct answer for every input is always computable. This lets us measure not just accuracy, but structural alignment.

Task Setup

Language & Data

Sequences generated from DFAs over a regular language alphabet.

  • Alphabet: {a, b, c}, d optional
  • Training: positive sequences only
  • Objective: next-token prediction
  • Train: short sequences (≤ 5)
  • OOD: longer sequences (6–14)
Models

GPT-Style Transformers

Two model sizes trained and compared on identical data regimes.

  • Small model: 128 hidden, 2 layers
  • Large model: 256 hidden, 4 layers
  • Both trained via next-token prediction
  • Classification baseline included

Key Findings

Failure is structured, not random.

Models perform well on training-length sequences but degrade sharply and systematically on longer inputs, especially at sequence-termination tokens. This is not noise: it's evidence of a deeper architectural limitation.

01
Strong In-Distribution Performance

Models achieve low loss and stable perplexity on sequences similar to training data, confirming the experimental setup is sound and the task is learnable in-distribution.

Violates Q1 expectation of generalization
02
Sharp Out-of-Distribution Failure

Performance degrades sharply on longer sequences. The degradation is not gradual, it spikes at specific sequence lengths, indicating structured failure rather than general uncertainty.

Violates Q2 Inductive Bias
03
Structural Collapse at EOS

Failure concentrates specifically at end-of-sequence prediction, the token that depends directly on understanding the DFA's accepting states. Token prediction stays stable; EOS collapses.

Violates Q3 State-Respecting Bias
04
Scaling Doesn't Fix It

Increasing model size (from 128→256 hidden, 2→4 layers) improves in-distribution performance but does not resolve OOD generalization failure, ruling out capacity as the bottleneck.

Violates Q4 Extrapolative Predictability
Key Insight

A model has learned the underlying rule if and only if its predictions factor through the state mapping. Otherwise, it is learning a spurious function over strings, not the true generative structure.

m̂_D(x) ≈ g ∘ φ(x)

Transformers trained via next-token prediction do not learn functions of this form. Instead, they rely on surface-level features-sequence length, token position, local correlations-that work within the training distribution but break at its boundaries.

Implications

What this means for the future of machine learning.

These findings are not just about regular languages. They point to fundamental questions about what transformer-based architectures are actually learning, and where that matters most.

⚠

Reliability Under Distribution Shift

If models learn heuristics rather than rules, their behavior in deployment scenarios that differ from training data is fundamentally unpredictable, not just uncertain.

🔍

Interpretability Gaps

High accuracy can mask a total absence of structural understanding. Standard benchmarks are insufficient to distinguish rule learning from pattern approximation.

🏗

Architectural Limitations

Scaling existing architectures does not close the generalization gap. Solving this likely requires inductive biases built into the architecture itself, not just more parameters or data.

Future Work

Where this research points next.

The DFA framework opens a controlled experimental space that can be extended in several directions: each targeting a different dimension of the generalization problem.
